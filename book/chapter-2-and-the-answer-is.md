# Chapter 2: And the Answer is?



In 1885, James Frazer began work on The Golden Bough and didn't publish the
twelve-volume edition until 1915. A thirteenth volume came out in 1936. That's a
fifty-year obsession. This makes me feel much better about this project.


Frazer's quest was sparked by a seemingly simple question: How was power transferred at the Sanctuary of Diana at Nemi, where priest-kings could only gain the position by murdering their predecessor? Five decades and twelve volumes later, he had his answer.


My journey started with a similarly simple question. I had heard Habitat for Humanity's claim that kids raised in homes their parents owned did better in school than kids raised in rented homes. (Spoiler alert: they do.) I wanted to see the data behind this claim. And with that, I was off on to my own little Golden Bough quest. The PSID turned out to be the only publicly available dataset I could find that could answer it. 


Here is the proof for Habitat's claim. In the regression analysis output below, White males with renting parents serve as the reference group (baseline). The reference group receives on average 13.173 years of education. Some college.

![]()


Kids whose parents own their homes go to school an additional 0.985 years,
arriving at 14.158 years. Perhaps an Associate's Degree.


How can we be so certain that kids whose parents own their own homes do better?  Because of the three asterisks. This tells us that, after examining thousands of families, the probability that we would see a difference this large purely by chance — if home ownership had no real effect — is extremely small, as in one in a thousand small.


And there we have it. Kids whose parents own their home get 0.985 years more
schooling. That is almost one year. Anyone inclined to see the full Regression Report can click here.
